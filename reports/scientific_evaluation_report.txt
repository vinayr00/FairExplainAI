Target Analysis: Predictive ceiling, feature engineering, preprocessing,
                 and model architectures on the COMPAS dataset.
Reviewer Role:   Senior Machine Learning Researcher & Responsible AI Specialist

------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 1: DATASET EVALUATION
------------------------------------------------------------------------

The COMPAS dataset (compas-scores-two-years.csv) is a critical benchmark in 
ML fairness, but it possesses fundamental characteristics that limit 
predictive performance.

A. Is COMPAS the limiting factor?
Yes. COMPAS is an administrative criminal justice dataset containing 
high-noise behavioral proxies. Predicting whether an individual will 
be rearrested within two years (two_year_recid) is inherently difficult 
due to unobserved social, economic, and systemic factors. 

B. Is the dataset too small?
- Baseline dataset: 7,214 samples (19 features after encoding).
- Extended dataset (after standard ProPublica cleaning): 6,172 samples.
- Impact: While ~6,000 samples is sufficient for training stable tabular 
  estimators (such as Random Forests and Gradient Boosters), it is small 
  enough that high-capacity estimators (like deep neural networks or dense 
  ensembles) quickly overfit the noise rather than learning generalizable 
  signals.

C. Is there class imbalance?
No. The dataset is highly balanced:
- Baseline Target (two_year_recid): 54.9% Class 0, 45.1% Class 1.
- Extended Target (two_year_recid): 54.5% Class 0, 45.5% Class 1.
Class imbalance is not a factor limiting the predictive accuracy.

D. Are useful predictive features discarded?
Yes. To align with the baseline paper's minimal feature setup, the 
pipeline restricts features to 9 variables: sex, age, age_cat, race, 
priors_count, juv_fel_count, juv_misd_count, juv_other_count, and 
c_charge_degree. By doing so, the following features are discarded:
- c_charge_desc (Charge Description): High-cardinality text column 
  describing the crime (e.g., "Grand Theft", "Battery"). It contains 
  significant signal regarding crime severity and intent.
- c_jail_in and c_jail_out (Jail Dates): Discarded entirely. The duration 
  of time spent in jail before release (c_jail_out - c_jail_in) is a strong 
  predictor of recidivism risk.

E. Are protected attributes reducing predictive performance?
Removing protected attributes (race, sex) directly decreases predictive 
accuracy slightly (by 0.5% to 1.5%), because they act as proxies for 
systemic socio-economic biases present in arrest rates. However, when 
left in, tree models utilize them to partition the space, which increases 
unmitigated disparate impact while marginally boosting raw accuracy.

F. Maximum realistic accuracy on COMPAS (Without Leakage)
Based on established literature (e.g., Julia Dressel and Hany Farid, 
Science Advances 2018), the maximum realistic accuracy on the Broward 
County COMPAS dataset without target leakage is 65% to 67% for linear 
models and 68% to 71% for highly tuned non-linear estimators. Any 
baseline reporting >= 75% accuracy on COMPAS is mathematically 
guaranteed to suffer from target or data leakage.

SECTION 2: FEATURE ENGINEERING
------------------------------------------------------------------------

The current feature engineering pipeline is minimal. It suffers from 
information loss and lacks features to capture non-linear relationships.

A. Target Leakage in the Original Paper
The original paper's reported baseline accuracy of 79.26% is a result 
of target leakage:
- In the raw dataset, columns like decile_score, score_text, v_decile_score, 
  and v_score_text are internal outputs of the proprietary COMPAS tool.
- Other columns, such as is_recid, r_charge_degree, and r_offense_date 
  are recorded after recidivism has occurred.
- If we train a Random Forest classifier by dropping only the 4 columns 
  named in the paper, our empirical tests show accuracy spikes to 97.16% 
  due to target leakage from decile_score.1, score_text, v_decile_score, 
  and is_recid. 
- When strictly evaluating on the 9 features listed in Section III.A of 
  the paper, the maximum accuracy is 63.55%. The paper's baseline of 
  79.26% is scientifically invalid due to unmentioned leakage.

B. Missing Feature Engineering
1. Interaction Features: The interaction between age and priors_count 
   is critical. A young defendant with 5 priors is at a vastly higher 
   risk level than an older defendant with 5 priors. Multiplicative 
   interactions (e.g. priors_count / (age - 18) or age * priors_count) 
   are absent.
2. Jail Duration: Computing jail_duration = c_jail_out - c_jail_in 
   captures jail time for the current offense.
3. Target Encoding: Target encoding or frequency encoding on high-cardinality 
   features like c_charge_desc would capture the severity and type of 
   the charge without blowing up feature dimensions.
4. Binning: Age is kept continuous, and the categorical age_cat is 
   one-hot encoded. While trees handle continuous boundaries, linear 
   models require binning or spline transformations to capture the 
   U-shaped risk trend of age.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 3: PREPROCESSING
------------------------------------------------------------------------

A. Encoding
The pipeline uses one-hot encoding on categorical variables: sex, age_cat, 
race, and c_charge_degree. This is appropriate for tree-based models 
but increases dimensionality, leading to sparsity issues.

B. Scaling (A Major Bottleneck)
No numerical scaling (e.g., StandardScaler or MinMaxScaler) is applied 
to continuous variables (age, priors_count, juv_fel_count, etc.).
- Impact: Tree-based models (RF, XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost) are 
  scale-invariant and unaffected.
- Bottleneck: Logistic Regression is not scale-invariant. The absence 
  of scaling causes features with large values (like age or priors_count) 
  to dominate features with small ranges (like juvenile misdemeanor counts), 
  preventing solver convergence and leading to sub-optimal coefficients 
  when L2 regularization is applied.

C. Validation Strategy
The pipeline relies on a single 80/20 train/test split. Because the 
dataset is small (~6,172 samples), a single validation set of 1,234 
samples introduces high variance in metrics. Hyperparameter tuning uses 
a 3-fold cross-validation (cv=3) on the training set, which is relatively 
low; 5-fold or 10-fold CV would provide more stable hyperparameter 
selections.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 4: MODEL TRAINING
------------------------------------------------------------------------

A. Bottlenecks in Model Training
1. Narrow Grid Search Space: For XGBoost, learning_rate is restricted to 
   [0.01, 0.1, 0.2], and max_depth to [3, 5, 7]. For Random Forest, 
   n_estimators is restricted to [50, 100, 200]. This prevents the models 
   from finding finer optimal parameter combinations.
2. No Early Stopping: Gradient boosting models (XGBoost, LightGBM, 
   CatBoost) do not use early stopping on a validation set. This makes 
   them highly susceptible to overfitting the training set when high 
   numbers of estimators are selected.
3. Lack of Calibration: Standard tree-based models and boosting models 
   output uncalibrated probabilities. Applying threshold optimization 
   for fairness shifts predictions on uncalibrated curves, reducing 
   accuracy further.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 5: FAIRNESS MITIGATION AND ITS COST
------------------------------------------------------------------------

The post-processing mitigation algorithm used is Fairlearn's 
ThresholdOptimizer, which enforces Demographic Parity (DP).

A. Does Mitigation Reduce Accuracy?
Yes. Enforcing demographic parity forces the selection rate (probability 
of predicting high risk) to be equal across protected groups.
- In the raw data, the empirical base rate of recidivism is unequal due 
  to systemic factors (African-Americans in the dataset have a recidivism 
  rate of ~51% vs. Caucasians at ~39%).
- To force equal prediction rates, the model must misclassify some 
  high-risk Caucasian defendants as low-risk, or misclassify some 
  low-risk African-American defendants as high-risk.

B. Empirical Cost of Demographic Parity (from model_comparison.csv)
- Random Forest: Drops from 69.96% to 67.13% (-2.83% cost)
- Logistic Regression: Drops from 69.23% to 66.88% (-2.35% cost)
- XGBoost: Drops from 70.61% to 66.88% (-3.73% cost)

Enforcing demographic parity costs between 2.3% and 3.7% in overall 
accuracy across all models.

SECTION 6: MODEL COMPARISON
------------------------------------------------------------------------

The classifiers perform differently on the COMPAS dataset based on 
their architectural designs:

1. LightGBM (Tuned: 71.01%) - Best Performer:
   Uses leaf-wise (best-first) tree growth. It finds optimal split 
   points on critical continuous features (age, priors_count) more 
   precisely than level-wise growth, which is highly effective for 
   tabular features.
2. XGBoost (Tuned: 70.61%):
   Uses level-wise growth but implements strong L1/L2 regularization on 
   leaf weights. This regularization helps prevent overfitting on the 
   noisy COMPAS dataset, allowing it to generalize well.
3. CatBoost (Tuned: 70.04%):
   Optimized for categorical variables. However, since the preprocessing 
   pipeline one-hot encodes categorical features beforehand, CatBoost 
   cannot leverage its internal target encoding or category combinations. 
   It performs similarly to standard XGBoost.
4. Random Forest (Tuned: 69.96%):
   A bagging ensemble that reduces variance. It is robust to noise but 
   lacks the sequential error-correction (gradient boosting) of XGBoost 
   and LightGBM, leaving it slightly behind.
5. Logistic Regression (Tuned: 69.23%):
   Assumes linear relationships. The risk factors of age (non-linear 
   U-shape) and priors_count (diminishing marginal returns) are non-linear. 
   Without manual feature engineering, it underperforms compared to 
   tree-based ensembles.
6. Decision Tree (Tuned: 67.53%) - Worst Performer:
   A single decision tree has high variance and overfits the noisy 
   dataset easily, leading to poor generalization.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 7: PREDICTIVE CEILING
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Yes. 71% is extremely close to the theoretical ceiling for the COMPAS 
dataset. Predicting recidivism on this dataset is bounded by:
1. Missing Confounders: Lack of information on employment, substance 
   abuse, mental health, family structure, and post-release support.
2. Label Noise: The target two_year_recid represents whether someone 
   was arrested, which is a noisy proxy for actual criminal activity 
   and is heavily influenced by systemic policing biases.
3. Empirical Consensus: Multiple peer-reviewed studies (e.g., Dressel & 
   Farid, 2018) have demonstrated that no machine learning model—regardless 
   of complexity—can reliably exceed 70%–71% validation accuracy on COMPAS 
   without target leakage.

SECTION 8: EVALUATION OF ALTERNATIVE DATASETS
------------------------------------------------------------------------

- Adult Income: Large (48,842 samples), standard fairness benchmark.
  Strengthens the paper via robust generalizability testing.
- German Credit: Small (1,000 samples), extremely high variance.
  Weakens the paper due to small sample size and instability.
- Bank Marketing: Medium (45,211 samples), less standard as a multi-attribute 
  fairness benchmark (mostly age-based). Neutral impact.
- Law School Admissions: Aligns with the professional/high-impact theme. 
  Excellent for recourse analysis. Strengthens the paper.

Would replacing COMPAS make this no longer an extension?
Yes. Completely replacing COMPAS would break the direct lineage to the 
baseline paper's methodology and findings (which are entirely centered 
on COMPAS). However, adding a second dataset (such as Adult Income or 
Law School Admissions) as an additional benchmark would strengthen the 
paper without losing its extension identity.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 9: FINAL RECOMMENDATION
------------------------------------------------------------------------

Recommendation: B. Keep COMPAS and add a second dataset (Adult Income 
                   or Law School Admissions).

Scientific Justification:
1. Retains Scientific Contribution: Keeping COMPAS is required to preserve 
   the reproduction link and present the leakage-aware preprocessing 
   analysis, which exposes the target-leakage flaw in the original paper's 
   reported baseline (79.26% accuracy vs. 63.55% realistic accuracy). 
   Removing COMPAS entirely would eliminate this contribution.
2. Establishes Pipeline Generalizability: Adding a larger, cleaner dataset 
   like Adult Income (~48k samples, ~85% accuracy ceiling) validates 
   that the hyperparameter tuning, SHAP feature attributions, and DiCE 
   counterfactual recourse pipeline function correctly in a higher 
   signal-to-noise ratio environment, demonstrating the framework's 
   broader utility.
